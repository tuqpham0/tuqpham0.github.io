My research is in topological data analysis and computational geometry, especially in settings motivated by machine learning. This page gives a compact overview of two threads of that work and points to papers that contain the full technical details.

## Persistent Homology
- ["Computing Representatives of Persistent Homology Generators with a Double Twist"](https://arxiv.org/abs/2403.04100) - P., Wagner

We avoid explaining the entire machinery for persistent homology, instead giving a broad overview of the PH pipeline, our goal, and our methodology.

Loosely speaking, (1-parameter) persistent homology captures the 'holes' in your space. These holes are topological features of a space. For example, a circle has a 1-dimensional hole while a figure 8 has two 1-dimensional holes. A sphere captures a 2-dimensional hole and a torus captures a 2-dimensional hole whose boundary can be paramaterized with two 1-dimensional holes. There are higher dimensional analogues, often called 'voids'. Persistent homology captures this by constructing a filtration of spaces on discrete data, determined by a parameter, and then reducing the generated boundary matrix.

The standard output of persistent homology is the persistence diagram. This diagram captures at what parameter values do these topological features appear and when they disappear, and thus, how *persistent* this feature was within the data. However, we for each feature, we can also capture *where* these features appear in the data during their lifetime. The representatives of persistent homology generators provide a geometric visualization of where these holes are in the data.

We introduce a meta algorithm for computing persistent homology generators when the high dimensional simplices dominate the filtration. It can be implemented in any preexisting software, utilizing the provided algorithms. Our method is experimentally shown to be over 200$\times$ faster than the standard algorithm for computing these representative cycles in PHAT.

Virk and {\v C}ufar independently devised this algorithm and implemented [Ripserer](https://mtsch.github.io/Ripserer.jl/dev/) for Vietoris--Rips complexes in Ripser.

## Bregman Divergences
- ["Fast Kd-Trees for the Kullback–Leibler Divergence and Other Decomposable Bregman Divergences"](https://arxiv.org/abs/2502.13425) - P., Wagner
- ["Bregman-Hausdorff divergence: strengthening the connections between computational geometry and machine learning"](https://arxiv.org/abs/2504.07322) - P. Dal Poz Kou{\v r}imsk{\`a}, Wagner

As Bregman divergences are generated by functions of Legendre type, we start our definitions from there.

**Definition** Let $\Omega\subset\mathbb{R}^k$ be an open convex set. A function $F:\Omega\to \mathbb{R}$ is of *Legendre type* if:
   1. $F$ is differentiable
   2. $F$ is strictly convex
   3. If $\partial \Omega\neq\varnothing$, then $\lim_{x\to\partial\Omega}\|\nabla F(x)\|=\infty$

Often, the third condition is omitted, but it is essential for the Legendre transform.

**Definition** For a function of Legendre type $F$, the *Bregman divergence* generated is:
\[D_F:\Omega\times \Omega\to [0,\infty]\qquad\qquad D_F(x\|y)= F(x)-(F(y) + \langle\nabla F(y), x-y\rangle\]

This is the vertical distance between $F(x)$ and the approximation of $F(x)$ given by the linear approximation of $F$ at $y$.

**Example** In machine learning and information theory, a popular dissimilarity measurement between probability distributions is relative entropy, also known as the Kullback--Leibler divergence. This is a member of the Bregman divergences, and its definition falls neatly into pre-existing information theoretic terminology.
\begin{align*}
   D_{KL}(x\|y)   &=\sum_{i=1}^k x_i\log_2 \frac{x_i}{y_i}\\
                  &=-\sum_{i=1}^k x_i\log_2 \frac{1}{x_i} - \sum_{i=1}^{k}x_i\log_2\frac{1}{y_i}
                  &=-\text{entropy} - (-\text{cross entropy})
                  &= \text{cross entropy} - \text{entropy}
\end{align*}
In this example, we have the entropy at a distribution, $\eta(x)$, and the cross entropy $\eta(x,y)$ is the approximation of $\eta(x)$ given a linear approximation of $\eta$ at $y$. In information theory, entropy is concave, so extra negatives are introduced to fit the standard Bregman definition.

Other popular examples of Bregman divergences include:
 - Squared Euclidean distance;
 - Itakura--Saito divergence; Useful as a loss function for signal processing tasks
 - Mahalanobis divergences; Often defined with covariance matrices from statistics

In particular, Bregman divergences are *not* metrics. Only the subfamily of Mahalanobis Bregman divergences can be symmetric, and they never satisfy the triangle inequality.

Despite the lack of metric features, Bregman divergences permit a well-behaved geometry. We posit that when training a machine learning model, the loss function determines the geometry on the output space. For example, when training a classifier using cross entropy, one is using an shortcut for the KL divergence. Thus to analyse model output, one should use the geometry induced by the KL divergence.

The Bregman Kd-tree is an extension of the metric tree that can compute both exact and approximate $k$-nearest neighbours. It can additionally quickly compute the Bregman--Hausdorff divergence, a measurement of divergence between *sets* of *unpaired* vectors. This concept is useful in generative models where generated data is not explicitly matched with training data.

Bregman Kd-trees are more efficient than other competing public portable implementations and are available at the [BANN GitHub repository](https://github.com/tuqpham0/BANN). The package is also installable with pip and can be found on [PyPI](https://pypi.org/project/BANN/).
